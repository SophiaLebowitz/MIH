Mandatory Inclusionary Housing in Brooklyn Does Little for the Rent Burdened

By Sophia Lebowitz

Sunset Park is in the middle of a rezoning fight, Totem, a Brooklyn based developer wants to turn this Dunkin Doughnuts into a mixed use apartment building with 35 permanently affordable units using MIH guidelines.

Mandatory Inclusionary Housing, MIH, was created by Mayor De Blasio in 2016 as a part of his Housing New York plan. The plan’s objective was to “require through zoning actions a share of new housing to be permanently affordable,” according to New York City’s Department of Planning.

The affordability of the units is determined by the Average Median Income (AMI) of the entire city, which is currently about $100,000 for a family of four.

David Estrada, former chief of staff for Council Member Carlos Menchaca and current director of the Sunset Park 5th Ave. BID, said “MIH is a broken system, fixing it would take 3-10 years.”There were only two community districts in Brooklyn with a median income of over $100,000 last year.

Some community members don’t want to settle for a new development that has any less than 100% affordable housing. Estrada said he thinks this project, considering our current system, wouldn’t do any harm, “we don't want anything until we get everything? That’s no way to run a city.”
